## Design Context

### Users
Freelance SEO consultants, independent developers, and semi-technical practitioners who audit websites for clients or their own projects. Skill level ranges from SEO specialist to "knows enough to identify what's broken." They use the tool to do a job — they're not exploring, they're diagnosing. Speed and clarity of findings matter more than onboarding or hand-holding.

### Brand Personality
**Surgical and unforgiving.** Three words: precise, direct, ruthless. The interface should feel like a tool built by someone who has zero patience for wasted space or ambiguity. Every element earns its place. Nothing decorates; everything informs.

### Aesthetic Direction
- Dark-first (default dark — confirmed for the use context: focused technical sessions, often screen-intensive work)
- NOT toy-like: no soft pastels, no rounded bubbly UI, no playful micro-copy
- NOT old-school: no table-heavy layouts, no gray gradient headers, no 2012 SaaS dashboard energy
- Existing fonts Manrope + Syne are established — respect them
- Existing accent colors: green `#00ff88` (success/brand) and blue `#4d8dff` (interactive) — respect them
- Lean toward editorial sharpness over UI softness

### Emotional Goal
When a crawl finishes and results appear, the user should feel **empowered and clear-headed** — like they have complete sight of every problem and a concrete path to fix it. Not overwhelmed, not impressed by the tool itself — just ready to act. The interface should get out of the way and put the problems front and center.

### Design Principles
1. **Data is the hero.** The UI exists to surface findings, not to decorate them. Remove any element that competes with the data for attention.
2. **Hierarchy through ruthless contrast.** Critical issues must look critical at a glance — weight, size, and color used intentionally, not decoratively.
3. **No filler copy.** Labels, eyebrows, headings — every word must be load-bearing. If a label restates what's visually obvious, cut it.
4. **Friction only where it prevents disaster.** Destructive actions get confirmation. Everything else should be one action away.
5. **Surgical over polished.** A sharp, precise interaction beats a smooth but vague one. The tool should feel like a scalpel, not a spa.
